Nevada Personal Injury Law:
What Every Accident Victim Needs to Know

Las Vegas, Nevada · March 2026 · 9 min read

Nevada's personal injury laws directly affect what you can recover, how much you can recover, and how long you have to act. Most accident victims have no idea these rules exist until it's too late to do anything about them. This guide covers the essential laws in plain language — not to replace legal advice, but to make sure you're not blindsided.

The Statute of Limitations

NRS 11.190
2 Years for Personal Injury Claims
In Nevada, you have exactly 2 years from the date of the accident to file a personal injury lawsuit. If you miss this deadline — by even one day — you permanently lose the right to sue, regardless of how clear the liability or how serious the injuries. The clock does not pause because you're negotiating with insurance.
Important: The 2-year clock runs from the date of the accident, not the date you discovered your injuries. For property damage claims, the limit is 3 years.
Don't assume you have time. Most people who miss the statute of limitations thought they had more time than they did. Insurance negotiations can drag on for months, creating a false sense of progress while the deadline approaches.

Modified Comparative Negligence

NRS 41.141
The 51% Rule
Nevada uses a modified comparative fault system. If you are partially at fault for the accident, your recovery is reduced by your percentage of fault. However, if you are found to be 51% or more at fault, you cannot recover anything at all.
Example: Your damages are $100,000. A jury finds you were 20% at fault. You recover $80,000. If that same jury found you 51% at fault, you would recover nothing.

This is why what you say at the scene — and to the insurance adjuster — matters so much. Any statement that suggests partial fault can be used to reduce or eliminate your recovery. Insurance companies specifically train adjusters to elicit these admissions.

Nevada Minimum Insurance Requirements

NRS 485.185
Minimum Liability Coverage
Nevada requires all drivers to carry minimum liability insurance of: $25,000 per person for bodily injury · $50,000 per accident for bodily injury · $20,000 for property damage. These minimums are far below what serious accident injuries can cost. Many serious injury cases exceed these limits significantly.
When damages exceed the at-fault driver's policy limits, your own underinsured motorist (UIM) coverage becomes critical. Always know your own UIM limits.

Uninsured and Underinsured Motorist Coverage

NRS 687B.145
UM/UIM Protections
Nevada requires insurance companies to offer uninsured and underinsured motorist coverage. If you're hit by a driver with no insurance or insufficient coverage, your own UM/UIM coverage steps in. Critically, Nevada law prohibits insurers from reducing UM/UIM coverage by the amount recovered from the at-fault driver — you can potentially collect from both.
If the at-fault driver has $25,000 coverage and you have $100,000 UIM coverage, you may be able to collect the $25,000 from their insurance and then access your UIM for additional recovery up to your policy limits.

Punitive Damages

NRS 42.005
When Extra Damages Are Available
Nevada allows punitive damages in cases where the defendant acted with oppression, fraud, or malice — meaning intentional or reckless disregard for others' rights. Drunk driving cases often qualify. Nevada caps punitive damages at $300,000 or 3 times the compensatory damages, whichever is greater.
Punitive damages are relatively rare — they require a specific showing of misconduct beyond mere negligence. But in cases involving DUI or willful recklessness, they can significantly increase the total recovery.

Dram Shop Liability

NRS 41.1305
Liability for Serving Alcohol
Nevada's dram shop law holds alcohol vendors liable when they serve alcohol to a visibly intoxicated person who then causes an accident. In Las Vegas — with its casino and nightclub culture — this law is particularly relevant. If the at-fault driver was served at a casino, bar, or restaurant, that establishment may share liability.
This can dramatically increase available insurance coverage and total recovery in DUI cases, as commercial establishments typically carry much larger liability policies than individual drivers.

What These Laws Mean for Your Case

Every one of these laws requires strategic navigation. The statute of limitations means time is not your friend — inaction has permanent consequences. Comparative negligence means how the accident is characterized matters enormously. Insurance minimums mean you need to know your own coverage as well as the other driver's. And the availability of dram shop claims in Las Vegas means some cases have recovery options that most victims never knew existed.
