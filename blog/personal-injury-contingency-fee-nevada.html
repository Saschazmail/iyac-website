How Contingency Fees Work
in Nevada Personal Injury Cases

Las Vegas, Nevada · March 2026 · 8 min read

"No fee unless we win" is the most repeated promise in PI advertising. It's true — but it's incomplete. Understanding the full fee structure, including case costs, the difference between gross and net settlement, and how the math actually works is critical before you sign a retainer. This guide explains it all in plain language.

The Basic Structure: Contingency Fee

Personal injury attorneys in Nevada work on contingency — meaning they receive a percentage of your settlement or verdict, and nothing if you lose. This structure exists because most accident victims can't afford hourly legal fees, and it aligns the attorney's incentive with yours: they only get paid when you do, and they get more when you get more.

Standard Nevada contingency rates:

33.33% if the case settles before trial · 40% if the case goes to trial · Some attorneys charge higher rates for complex cases or appeals — always confirm the rate in writing before signing

The Part Most People Miss: Case Costs

Separate from the attorney's fee are case costs — the actual out-of-pocket expenses of building your case. These are advanced by the attorney and reimbursed from your settlement. They include:

Medical records and bills · Police and accident reports · Expert witness fees (can be $5,000-$20,000+) · Court filing fees · Deposition costs and court reporter fees · Investigation and accident reconstruction · Mediation fees

The critical question that most clients never ask: do case costs come out before or after the attorney's percentage is calculated? The answer significantly affects what you net.

The Math — Two Different Scenarios

Scenario A: Costs Deducted Before Attorney's Fee
Gross settlement$100,000
Case costs− $10,000
Net after costs$90,000
Attorney fee (33% of $90k)− $29,700
You receive$60,300
Scenario B: Costs Deducted After Attorney's Fee
Gross settlement$100,000
Attorney fee (33% of $100k)− $33,333
Case costs− $10,000
You receive$56,667

The same settlement, the same fee percentage, the same costs — but a $3,633 difference in what you net. On larger cases, this difference can be $10,000 or more. Always ask which method applies and get it in writing.

What Happens to Costs If You Lose

Critical Question
If I lose, do I owe case costs?
This varies by attorney and by agreement. Some attorneys advance costs and forgive them on a loss — you owe nothing. Others expect reimbursement of costs even if the case is lost, meaning you could receive nothing from your case but still owe $5,000-$15,000 in costs. Confirm this explicitly before you sign.

Medical Liens: Another Deduction from Your Settlement

If you received medical treatment under a lien arrangement — where the provider agreed to wait for payment until your settlement — those lien balances are paid from your settlement before you receive your share. This is in addition to attorney fees and case costs.

Experienced PI attorneys negotiate lien reductions as part of their standard practice. A doctor's lien for $30,000 may be negotiated down to $18,000 — significantly improving your net. This is another area where attorney skill directly affects what you take home.

Why Attorney Fees Don't Mean You Net Less

The Insurance Research Council found that claimants represented by attorneys recover an average of 3.5 times more than unrepresented claimants — even after attorney fees. The attorney's 33% comes out of a significantly larger number than you would have negotiated alone.

An unrepresented claimant who accepts a $30,000 settlement when their case was worth $90,000 has "saved" the 33% fee — and lost $45,000 in the process. The math consistently favors representation.

Questions to Ask Before Signing

Based on everything above, these are the specific financial questions to ask every attorney:

What is your contingency percentage at settlement and at trial? · Do costs come out before or after your fee is calculated? · What do you estimate case costs will be for my type of case? · If I lose, do I owe costs? · Will you provide a written fee agreement before I sign anything? · Do you negotiate medical liens as part of your standard representation?
