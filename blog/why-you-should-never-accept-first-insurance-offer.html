Why You Should Never Accept
the First Insurance Offer

Las Vegas, Nevada · March 2026 · 7 min read

The call comes quickly. Often within 48 hours of the accident. An adjuster who sounds reasonable, even sympathetic. They've reviewed the claim. They're prepared to offer you something now to help you move forward. The number sounds real. It might even sound fair.

It almost certainly isn't.

Why the First Offer Is Always Too Low

Reason 01
You Don't Know the Full Extent of Your Injuries Yet
Soft tissue injuries, whiplash, and spinal trauma frequently don't fully manifest for days or weeks. An offer made before your injuries are fully documented cannot possibly account for future medical needs. Once you sign, those future costs come out of your own pocket.
Reason 02
The Offer Ignores Non-Economic Damages
Pain and suffering, emotional distress, loss of enjoyment of life, and impact on relationships are all legally compensable in Nevada. First offers typically address only current medical bills and property damage — the damages that are easy to quantify and easy to minimize.
Reason 03
It Doesn't Account for Lost Earning Capacity
If your injuries affect your ability to work — now or in the future — that loss is recoverable. Calculating it properly requires documentation that hasn't been assembled in the first 48 hours after your accident.
Reason 04
The Adjuster Knows Things You Don't
Insurance adjusters handle hundreds of claims per year. They know comparable case values, policy limits, liability exposure, and how Nevada courts have ruled on similar cases. You've handled zero. That asymmetry is exactly what the first offer exploits.
Reason 05
Timing Is a Deliberate Tactic
The 24-48 hour offer is not a coincidence. It arrives before you've spoken to an attorney, before you understand your rights, and while you're still in the disorienting shock of the accident. The financial pressure of immediate bills makes the offer feel more attractive than it is.

What to Do When the Offer Arrives

1
Do not respond immediately
Tell them you'll review the offer and follow up. You are not obligated to decide on the spot. Any pressure to decide immediately is a tactic.
2
Request a written breakdown of how the offer was calculated
You have the right to understand what the insurer is valuing and how. Vague offers without line-item explanation should always be challenged.
3
Get a consultation before you respond
Every reputable PI attorney in Las Vegas offers a free consultation. It costs you nothing and gives you the information you need to respond intelligently. This is exactly what we help you set up.
4
Do not sign a release of any kind
Signing a release — even a "partial" one — may waive your rights to future claims. Nothing should be signed without attorney review.
5
Continue medical treatment
Do not let negotiation interrupt your medical care. Gaps in treatment are used against you regardless of when they happen.

When You Might Consider an Early Offer

There are situations where an early settlement makes sense — specifically when damages are clearly within a narrow range, liability is clear, injuries are minor and have fully resolved, and an attorney has reviewed the offer and confirmed it's appropriate. This combination is rarer than the insurance company would have you believe.

The key phrase: an attorney has reviewed and confirmed. Not "the adjuster told you it was fair."

What Happens After You Involve an Attorney

The insurance company's behavior changes immediately when an attorney sends a letter of representation. Offers improve. Communication becomes more formal and careful. Delays become less common because the insurer knows an attorney will escalate appropriately if they stall.
