How to Switch
Personal Injury Attorneys in Nevada

Las Vegas, Nevada · March 2026 · 7 min read

You have the absolute right to change your personal injury attorney at any point during your case. This is one of the most important things accident victims don't know — and that some attorneys actively discourage you from realizing. In Nevada, no contract or retainer agreement can take away this right.

The question isn't whether you can switch. It's whether you should — and how to do it without damaging your case.

Signs It May Be Time to Switch

You can't get anyone to return your calls or emails. If you haven't heard from your attorney or their staff in weeks — and they're not proactively updating you — that's a serious problem, not a temporary inconvenience.
You don't know who is working your case. If you have no idea who is handling the day-to-day and can't get a straight answer, your case may be buried in a high-volume practice.
Your attorney is pushing you to settle when you don't feel ready. Pressure to accept a settlement — especially before your medical treatment is complete — is a red flag. It may mean the attorney wants to close the case quickly rather than fight for full value.
You were handed to a junior associate or paralegal without explanation. You hired the attorney you met. If your case was transferred without your knowledge or consent, you have every right to address this or leave.
The strategy has changed without explanation. If your attorney's approach has shifted — especially toward settling when you expected litigation — you deserve a clear explanation. Silence on this point is not acceptable.
You simply lost trust. Trust is the foundation of the attorney-client relationship. You don't need a specific provable failure to justify switching. If you no longer trust your attorney to fight for you, that's sufficient reason.

Before You Switch: Have a Direct Conversation

Before making the decision to switch, have a frank conversation with your current attorney about your concerns. Sometimes what appears to be neglect is a temporary spike in caseload. Sometimes communication breakdowns are fixable. Sometimes the attorney's explanation for a strategic shift is entirely reasonable.

Request a face-to-face or video meeting. State your concerns directly. Observe how they respond — both the substance of the answer and whether they take your concerns seriously. If the conversation resolves your concerns, you may not need to switch. If it doesn't — or if they're defensive, dismissive, or unavailable for this conversation — you have your answer.

How to Switch: Step by Step

Step 01
Find your new attorney first
Identify and consult with a new attorney before notifying your current one. Make sure the new attorney is willing to take your case and understands the existing lien situation. We can help you identify the right replacement through our directory.
Step 02
Send a written termination notice
Write a brief letter or email to your current attorney stating that you are terminating the representation effective immediately. You don't need to provide reasons. Keep it simple and professional. Request that your file be transferred to your new attorney.
Step 03
Your new attorney handles the transition
Your new attorney will send a substitution of counsel form and contact the old attorney for your file. You typically don't need to be involved in this process beyond authorizing it. The new attorney has handled this many times.
Step 04
Understand the lien situation
Your former attorney may have a lien on your case for work performed. This doesn't prevent the switch — but it means they'll be entitled to compensation for their work when the case concludes. Your new attorney will factor this into the overall representation.
Timing Considerations

Switching attorneys is most straightforward in the early stages of a case. Once a trial date is set, courts may be less willing to accommodate a change in representation. If your case is approaching trial, your new attorney needs to be engaged immediately and will need to assess whether the timing works. Don't let timing concerns keep you in a bad representation situation — but be aware of them.

Will Switching Cost You More?

The fee structure is the same. You will not pay double attorney fees — you still pay one contingency percentage. That percentage gets split between the old and new attorney based on the work each performed. In most cases, you net the same amount you would have with only one attorney throughout.

The potential cost of switching is time — your new attorney needs to get up to speed on your case. For this reason, switching earlier in the process is better than switching later. But a case that's being mishandled is almost always better off with new representation, regardless of timing.
