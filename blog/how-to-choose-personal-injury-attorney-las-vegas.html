How to Choose a Personal Injury Attorney
in Las Vegas

Las Vegas, Nevada · March 2026 · 8 min read

Las Vegas has more personal injury attorneys per capita than almost any city in America. Billboards on every freeway. TV commercials during every news break. Jingles you'll remember long after you've forgotten the name of the attorney. The marketing budgets are enormous — and they tell you almost nothing about whether that attorney will actually fight for you.

This guide cuts through it. Here's what actually matters when choosing a PI attorney in Las Vegas — and the questions to ask before you sign anything.

Why the Biggest Billboards Don't Mean the Best Attorneys

The attorneys who spend the most on advertising are running high-volume practices. Volume means cases move quickly — which often means settlements happen faster than they should, for less than they're worth. An attorney who takes 400 cases a year and settles most of them in pre-litigation is not the same as an attorney who takes 80 cases and fights each one until it's right.

Neither model is inherently wrong. But you should know which one you're getting — and choose deliberately.

The 7 Questions to Ask Every Attorney

Question 01
Who will actually work on my case day-to-day?
At large firms, senior partners sign cases and hand them to associates or paralegals. This isn't inherently bad — experienced associates can be excellent. But you deserve to know who you'll be talking to, who's making decisions, and who to call with questions.
Red flag: Vague answer. "Our team" is not an answer. You want a name.
Question 02
How many cases like mine have you handled — and what were the outcomes?
A car accident involving a tourist with a rental car is different from a commercial truck accident on I-15 is different from a slip and fall at a casino. Specific experience matters. General "personal injury" experience is a starting point, not a sufficient answer.
Red flag: They only reference total cases or total dollars recovered without specifics.
Question 03
Are you willing to take my case to trial if needed?
Insurance companies track which attorneys actually go to trial. An attorney with a known trial record gets better settlement offers because the insurer knows they can't lowball and walk away. Many PI attorneys rarely or never go to trial. Know which kind you're hiring.
Red flag: Hesitation, or pivoting immediately to "most cases settle."
Question 04
What is your honest assessment of my case value — and your reasoning?
A good attorney gives you a realistic range with specific reasoning: policy limits, liability percentage, injury severity, comparable cases. An attorney who gives you a high number without explaining it is telling you what you want to hear, not what you need to know.
Red flag: An unrealistically high number in the first consultation with no factual basis.
Question 05
How and how often will you communicate with me?
Communication is the number one complaint about PI attorneys. Get specifics: Who do I contact with questions? What is your response time standard? Will you proactively update me even when nothing has changed? Get this in writing if possible.
Red flag: "We'll be in touch as things develop" without specifics.
Question 06
What exactly comes out of my settlement — and when?
Standard contingency is 33% if settled, 40% if tried. But there are also case costs — filing fees, expert witnesses, medical records. The crucial question is whether these costs come off before or after the attorney's percentage. The difference can be thousands of dollars.
Red flag: Vague answer about "standard fees" without explaining the cost structure.
Question 07
What happens if I'm not satisfied with how my case is being handled?
You always have the right to switch attorneys. Your original attorney may have a lien for work already performed. Understanding this upfront — before you sign — prevents surprises later. Good attorneys answer this question directly and without defensiveness.
Red flag: Defensiveness, or implying you can't leave.

The Qualities That Actually Predict Results

Beyond the seven questions, the characteristics consistently correlated with good attorney-client outcomes — based on client testimonials across hundreds of Nevada cases — are:

Communication frequency. Not waiting for you to call. Proactive updates even when nothing has changed. This is the number one driver of client satisfaction regardless of case outcome.

Honesty about case weakness. An attorney who tells you what could go wrong is protecting you. An attorney who only tells you what you want to hear is managing you.

Medical coordination. The best PI attorneys help you access the right medical care — not just whatever their referral network happens to send you to. Documented medical treatment is the backbone of case value.

Trial readiness as a philosophy. Even for attorneys who rarely go to trial, the willingness to do so — and the preparation that comes with it — produces better settlements.

Signs an Attorney Is Not Right for You

Promises a specific dollar amount in the first meeting · Pressures you to sign immediately · Can't tell you who will work your case · Doesn't explain the fee structure clearly · Hasn't handled cases similar to yours · Seems more interested in signing you than understanding your situation · You leave the consultation feeling more confused than when you arrived
