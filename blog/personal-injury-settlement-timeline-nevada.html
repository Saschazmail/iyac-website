How Long Does a
Personal Injury Case Take in Nevada?

Las Vegas, Nevada · March 2026 · 8 min read

This is one of the most common questions after an accident — and one of the least honestly answered. The truthful answer ranges from a few months to several years, depending on factors within and outside your control. Here's what actually determines the timeline, and what to expect at each stage.

The Realistic Timeline

Day 1
The Accident
Report to police. Seek immediate medical attention. Document everything. Contact Accident Undone or an attorney. Do not give a recorded statement to any insurance company yet.
Week 1–2
Attorney Engagement and Investigation
Your attorney sends letters of representation to all insurance companies. Investigation begins — police reports, witness statements, surveillance footage, medical records. Preserving evidence early is critical.
Months 1–6+
Medical Treatment
This phase lasts as long as it takes. Your attorney should not settle your case until you reach Maximum Medical Improvement (MMI) — the point at which your doctor can assess permanent effects. Settling before MMI is one of the most common and costly mistakes.
After MMI
Demand Letter and Negotiation
Once treatment is complete, your attorney compiles a demand package — medical records, bills, lost wage documentation, and a detailed demand letter. The insurance company typically has 30 days to respond. Negotiation begins.
+30–90 days
Settlement — or Litigation Decision
Most cases settle during negotiation — typically within 6 to 18 months of the accident for straightforward cases. If the insurance company won't offer fair value, your attorney files suit. This extends the timeline significantly but often produces better outcomes.
If litigated
Discovery, Mediation, and Trial
Litigation in Nevada typically adds 12 to 24 months. Discovery — depositions, expert witnesses, document production — takes most of that time. Most litigated cases still settle before trial, often during court-ordered mediation.

What Speeds Cases Up

Clear liability
Rear-end collisions, red-light violations with witnesses, and other cases where fault is not contested move significantly faster.
Consistent medical treatment with no gaps
Cases with complete, continuous medical documentation from day one are more defensible and reach MMI more efficiently.
Adequate insurance limits
Cases where damages are clearly within the at-fault driver's policy limits resolve faster than those requiring underinsured motorist claims or multiple party negotiations.

What Slows Cases Down

Disputed liability
When both parties claim the other was at fault, investigation and potentially litigation is required before any resolution is possible.
Serious or permanent injuries
More serious injuries require longer treatment, more expert testimony, and more time to accurately calculate future costs. This is appropriate — settling prematurely on a serious injury claim is a significant mistake.
Insurance company bad faith
Some insurers deliberately delay to pressure settlement. Recognizing this pattern early — and responding with litigation threats — is where attorney experience matters most.

The Nevada Statute of Limitations

You have 2 years from the date of the accident to file a personal injury lawsuit in Nevada. Missing this deadline means losing your right to compensation entirely — regardless of how strong your case is. Don't let the timeline run while you're deciding what to do.

Why "Faster" Isn't Always Better

Insurance companies want to settle fast — before you understand your injuries and before an attorney gets involved. An offer that arrives in the first week is almost always the lowest offer they'll ever make. A case that resolves appropriately, on your timeline, is almost always worth more than one that resolves quickly on theirs.
