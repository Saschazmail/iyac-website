20 Questions to Ask a Personal Injury Attorney
Before You Hire Them

Las Vegas, Nevada · March 2026 · 10 min read

The initial consultation is your only opportunity to vet the attorney before you're locked in. Most people walk in unprepared and walk out having signed something before they understood what they were agreeing to. These 20 questions change that.

Print this page. Bring it. Ask every question. The way an attorney answers tells you as much as the answer itself.

Experience & Specialization
01
How many cases similar to mine have you handled in the past two years?
General PI experience is a starting point. Specific case type experience — truck accident, rideshare, slip and fall, casino injury — is what actually matters. The insurance companies and legal strategies for each are different.
What to listen for: Specific numbers and case types, not vague references to "many years of experience."
02
What percentage of your cases go to trial versus settling?
There's no right answer — but knowing their trial rate tells you something important about their approach. A very low trial rate may mean they settle cases too quickly. Insurance companies track this data.
What to listen for: Honest answer with context, not defensiveness.
03
Have you handled cases against the specific insurance company involved in my accident?
Different insurers have different strategies and settlement behaviors. An attorney who has fought Allstate dozens of times knows exactly how they operate. That knowledge has real value.
04
Are you a certified personal injury specialist in Nevada?
Nevada has a voluntary board certification process for PI specialists. It's not required, but attorneys who pursue it have demonstrated a level of commitment and expertise above the baseline.
Case Handling & Communication
05
Who will actually work on my case day-to-day — and what is their experience level?
At large firms, senior partners sign cases and hand them to associates or paralegals. This isn't inherently bad, but you deserve to know exactly who you're dealing with before you sign.
Red flag: "Our team" without a specific name.
06
What is your standard for returning client calls and messages?
Communication is the number one complaint about PI attorneys. Get a specific commitment — "within 24 hours" or "same business day." Vague answers predict poor communication.
07
How often will you proactively update me on my case — even when nothing has changed?
The "no news is good news" approach is the source of most client dissatisfaction. Good attorneys update clients on a defined schedule regardless of case activity.
08
How many active cases do you currently carry?
Caseload is a proxy for attention. An attorney managing 400 cases cannot give each one the same attention as one managing 80. There's no magic number, but their answer — and their honesty about it — is telling.
Fees & Financial Structure
09
What is your contingency fee percentage — at settlement and at trial?
Standard is 33% settled, 40% at trial. Some attorneys charge more. Know the number before you sign, not after.
10
How are case costs handled — and do they come out before or after your fee?
This is the question most people forget — and it can mean thousands of dollars. If costs come off before the attorney's percentage, you net more. If after, you net less. The difference on a $100,000 settlement can easily be $5,000-$10,000.
Ask for a written example with numbers. Any good attorney can produce one in 60 seconds.
11
What happens to your fee if my case is lost?
Contingency means no fee if you don't win — but case costs are a separate question. Some attorneys advance costs and forgive them on a loss; others expect repayment of costs regardless. Know this upfront.
12
Will you provide a written breakdown of all anticipated costs?
Expert witnesses, filing fees, medical records, depositions — these add up. A written estimate protects both parties and signals transparency.
Case Assessment & Strategy
13
What is your honest assessment of my case's strengths and weaknesses?
An attorney who only tells you the positives is managing you. An attorney who explains both sides is protecting you. The honest answer in the first consultation predicts the honest relationship throughout.
Red flag: Only positive framing with no acknowledgment of challenges.
14
What is a realistic settlement range — and what is the reasoning behind it?
You want a range with specific reasoning: policy limits, liability percentage, comparable cases, injury severity. A specific number without reasoning, especially a high one, is a sales tactic.
15
What factors could reduce the value of my case?
Pre-existing conditions, gaps in treatment, comparative fault, policy limits — these are the things that affect what you ultimately recover. You need to know them now, not during negotiation.
16
What is your strategy if the insurance company refuses to offer a fair settlement?
This question reveals trial willingness and litigation strategy. "We'll file suit" is not sufficient. You want to understand the escalation path and their comfort with it.
Rights & Exit Conditions
17
What happens if I want to switch attorneys?
You always have the right to switch. Your original attorney may have a lien for work performed. Understanding the mechanics upfront prevents surprises and reveals how the attorney handles this question under pressure.
Red flag: Defensiveness, or suggesting you can't leave.
18
Will you ever settle my case without my explicit approval?
In Nevada, an attorney cannot settle your case without your authorization. But this question confirms their understanding of client rights and reveals their approach to shared decision-making.
19
What should I not do while my case is pending?
Social media, recorded statements, gaps in treatment, discussing the case with others — these can harm your claim. A good attorney covers this proactively. If they don't raise it, ask it yourself.
20
Why should I choose you over the other attorneys I'm considering?
This is the most revealing question. The answer tells you what they believe their actual differentiator is — communication, trial record, specialization, personal attention. It also tells you how they handle direct questions under mild pressure. Listen carefully.
What to listen for: Specific, honest answer. Not "because we're the best."
