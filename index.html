The In Your Corner Score — Our Proprietary Rating System

Not all attorneys are created equal. After years of research, client interviews, and analysis of thousands of Nevada personal injury cases, our team developed the In Your Corner Score — a proprietary algorithm designed to cut through marketing budgets and surface the attorneys who actually deliver results for their clients.

Our scoring model evaluates each firm across dozens of weighted factors — from courtroom win rates and settlement averages to client communication scores and peer reputation. Among the key data sources we incorporate are Avvo (the legal industry's most comprehensive attorney rating platform), Martindale-Hubbell (the gold standard of peer review since 1868), and Super Lawyers (a rigorous peer-nomination process that selects less than 5% of attorneys in each state).

But our algorithm goes further. We factor in case specialization depth, trial willingness, staff-to-case ratios, response time data, and direct client feedback — because the attorney who wins the most awards isn't always the one who will fight hardest for your specific situation.

The result is a 1–5 star score that reflects not just reputation, but real-world performance. No attorney can pay to improve their IYC Score. It is earned — not bought.

The Legal Process

What Happens After
You Hire an Attorney

Most people have never needed a PI attorney before. Here's what to expect — no legal jargon.

1
Free Consultation

Every PI attorney on our list offers a free consultation. You meet, explain what happened, and they tell you if you have a case. No obligation. You pay nothing at this stage — ever.

2
Signing the Retainer

If you proceed, you sign a contingency fee agreement. This means your attorney only gets paid if you win — typically 33% of the settlement. You pay zero upfront.

3
Investigation & Medical Treatment

Your attorney gathers evidence, police reports, and witness statements. Meanwhile, your job is to follow your doctor's treatment plan — documented medical treatment is the backbone of your case.

4
Demand Letter & Negotiation

Once your treatment is complete, your attorney sends a demand letter to the insurance company. Negotiation begins. Most cases settle without going to trial — typically within 6–18 months.

5
Settlement or Trial

If a fair settlement is reached, you receive your check (minus attorney fees and medical liens). If not, your attorney takes the case to trial. The attorneys on our list are not afraid of either outcome.

Common Questions

Questions to Ask
Your Attorney

Before you sign anything, make sure you have answers to these.

Not all PI cases are the same. A car accident on the Strip involving a tourist is very different from a commercial truck accident on I-15. Ask specifically about their experience with your type of accident, injury severity, and insurance situation.
At larger firms, senior partners often sign cases but hand them to junior associates or paralegals. There's nothing wrong with this — but you deserve to know who will actually be working your case day-to-day. Ask directly.
Standard contingency fee is 33% if settled, often 40% if it goes to trial. But there are also case costs — filing fees, expert witnesses, medical records. Ask whether those costs come off the top before or after the attorney's percentage is calculated. The difference can be thousands of dollars.
A good attorney will give you a realistic range — not just a number to impress you. Ask for their reasoning: policy limits, liability percentage, injury severity, and comparable cases. If they give you an unrealistically high number in the consultation, that's a red flag.
Communication is the #1 complaint clients have about PI attorneys. Ask specifically: How often will I hear from you? Who do I call with questions? What is your typical response time? Getting this in writing as part of your retainer is completely reasonable.
You have the right to change attorneys at any time. However, your original attorney may have a lien on your case for work performed. Ask about this upfront. The good news: In Your Corner can help you navigate this if it ever comes to that — we're here throughout the process.
Insurance companies know which attorneys actually go to trial and which ones always settle. An attorney with a known willingness to go to court often gets better settlement offers. Ask about their trial experience with your type of case specifically.
